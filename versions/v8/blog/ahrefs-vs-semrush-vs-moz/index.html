Ahrefs starts at $129/mo for the Lite plan (previously $99 for the old "Lite" tier — Grandfathered users got a deal). The Standard plan ($249/mo) is where most agencies end up. Advanced runs $449/mo, and Enterprise is custom. The upside: Ahrefs rarely limits you on features within a tier; you hit limits on tracked keywords and projects instead.

SEMrush starts at $139.95/mo for the Pro plan. Guru ($249.95/mo) adds multi-device tracking, content marketing features, and historical data. Business ($499.95/mo) unlocks API access, extended limits, and share of voice analytics. SEMrush is the most expensive at entry level but arguably the broadest — you get competitive analysis, PPC tools, social media management, and SEO all in one place.

Moz Pro is easily the cheapest of the three at $49/mo for Standard. Medium ($99/mo) and Large ($179/mo) scale up the campaign and keyword limits. Moz also sells Moz Local (local SEO, $14/mo) and STAT (rank tracking, $199/mo) separately — the à-la-carte approach can get pricey if you need everything.

Bottom line: Moz wins on sticker price. SEMrush gives you the most features per dollar if you need PPC and social on top of SEO. Ahrefs sits in the middle — expensive but laser-focused on link data and content strategy.

Backlink Database: Index Size & Freshness

Backlinks remain a top-three Google ranking factor, and the size of a tool's link index directly affects how many opportunities (and threats) you can uncover.

Ahrefs built its reputation on backlinks. Its index covers roughly 40 trillion known links and is updated every 15–30 minutes for new discoveries. Ahrefs is widely considered the gold standard for link analysis — their "Link Intersect" and "Broken Link" reports are best-in-class. The interface makes it trivially easy to see a domain's link velocity, anchor text distribution, and the ratio of dofollow to nofollow links at a glance.

SEMrush claims a slightly larger index (~43 trillion links) and adds a historical backlink index spanning 35 trillion+ links going back years — incredibly useful for spotting link loss trends. SEMrush's "Backlink Gap" tool is excellent for competitive analysis, and the "Backlink Audit" tool helps disavow toxic links. Freshness is strong, typically within 24–48 hours of crawling.

Moz indexes roughly 40 trillion links via its Link Explorer. Moz's standout feature is Spam Score — a 1–100 rating that predicts how likely a domain is to be penalized by Google. It's not perfect, but it's a fantastic first-pass filter when vetting link prospects. The index is updated roughly weekly, which can feel slow compared to Ahrefs and SEMrush.

Keyword Research: Databases, Difficulty Scores & Clustering

Ahrefs has roughly 18 billion keywords across 243 countries. Its Keyword Explorer provides Keyword Difficulty (KD) scores from 0–100, estimated click-through rates for each position, and rich SERP overview data. The "Parent Topic" feature groups related keywords under broader topics — great for content clustering. The "Clicks" column (how many clicks a #1 result actually gets) sets Ahrefs apart from most competitors.

SEMrush claims the largest keyword database at 25+ billion keywords across 142 countries. The "Keyword Magic Tool" is the most intuitive keyword discovery interface of the three — you can filter by intent (informational, navigational, commercial, transactional), word count, and specific SERP features. SEMrush also offers organic and paid keyword difficulty scores, plus unique metrics like "Trends" that show search volume changes over time.

Moz Pro has the smallest keyword database (~500 million keywords) but compensates with a clear, beginner-friendly interface and solid Keyword Difficulty scores based on its own Domain Authority metric. Moz's Priority Score is a useful composite of volume, difficulty, and estimated clicks that helps you quickly identify low-effort, high-opportunity keywords.

Verdict: If raw database size is all that matters, SEMrush wins. If you value click-through estimation and content clustering, Ahrefs is stronger. If you're new to keyword research, Moz's simplicity is a genuine advantage.

Site Audit: Crawling Depth & Actionability

All three tools crawl your website and flag technical SEO issues — but the depth and quality of recommendations vary significantly.

Ahrefs' Site Audit is a marvel of clarity. It categorizes issues into errors, warnings, and notices, and groups them by type (HTTP, HTTPS, HTML, links, images, etc.). The crawl scheduler lets you run audits daily or weekly, and the "Crawl Stats" report shows how Googlebot sees your site vs. how Ahrefs sees it. The only real downside: Ahrefs limits the number of crawled pages based on your plan (100K on Lite, 500K on Standard, etc.).

SEMrush's Site Audit tracks issues over time with a historical graph, so you can see whether your site is getting healthier or sicker week over week. It surfaces the most critical issues first ("Top 5 Errors") and integrates with Google Analytics, Search Console, and its own On-Page SEO Checker for end-to-end recommendations. The 100,000-page crawl limit on Pro can feel restrictive for larger sites.

Moz Pro's Site Crawl is the most limited of the three. The Standard plan caps at 50 pages per campaign — that's tiny. Even the Medium plan ($99/mo) only allows 2,000 pages. Moz crawls more slowly and often misses JavaScript-rendered content. For small blogs, it's fine. For anything beyond 10,000 pages, you'll outgrow Moz quickly.

Ease of Use: Learning Curve & Interface Design

Ahrefs has a spartan interface that experienced SEOs love but beginners often find intimidating. The data density is high — every report surface tries to show you as much information as possible. The navigation is logical (Site Explorer, Keywords Explorer, Content Explorer, Site Audit), but the sheer depth of reports means a meaningful learning curve of 2–4 weeks before you feel fluent.

SEMrush has the busiest interface of the three — some might say cluttered. With tools for SEO, PPC, social media, content marketing, and competitive research all in one dashboard, it can feel overwhelming at first. That said, SEMrush's onboarding wizard and tooltips are excellent. Once you learn the layout (about 1–2 weeks), it's the most versatile platform of the three.

Moz Pro is the clear winner for ease of use. The interface is clean, modern, and focused. Metrics like Domain Authority (DA) and Page Authority (PA) are presented in a straightforward way that even a complete beginner can understand. Moz also has the best educational content of the three — their Beginner's Guide to SEO is legendary. Most users feel productive within a few days.

Final Verdict: Which One Should You Choose?

Pick Ahrefs if link building is your primary focus and you want the best content opportunity tools available. It's the choice of serious SEO professionals who need depth over breadth.

Pick SEMrush if you run both organic and paid campaigns, or if you want the largest keyword database and the richest competitive intelligence on the market.

Pick Moz Pro if you're new to SEO, managing a small site, or working on a tight budget. Moz's educational ecosystem and Spam Score make it a fantastic starting point.
